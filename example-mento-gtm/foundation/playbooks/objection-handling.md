# Objection Handling

> Mento's stance on objection handling: **never argue. Surface the underlying concern, address it with comparable-company evidence, and re-test fit.** If after that they're still not in, they're not in.

## The five recurring objections

### 1. "We're going to use Pave."

**The underlying concern:** safety — Pave is the obvious enterprise choice.

**The response:** Don't argue. Ask one question:

> *"What's the typical Pave land for a Series [B/C/D] at your headcount? Curious because we usually see them quote 4× our number when we win against them."*

If they share a number, you have a wedge. If they deflect, the deal is gone — set a 6-month follow-up.

### 2. "We just need spreadsheets for now."

**The underlying concern:** the trigger isn't salient enough yet.

**The response:** Don't push. Ask:

> *"Makes sense. What would have to be true for spreadsheets to feel like a problem in 60 days?"*

If they answer with a specific event (next board meeting, the CHRO's first review), set a follow-up *to that date*. If they answer abstractly, the trigger isn't there — wait for it.

### 3. "We don't have budget right now."

**The underlying concern:** could be true, could be a comfortable no.

**The response:** Test which one.

> *"If budget weren't the constraint, would the [trigger] you just told me about be a problem worth solving in the next 60 days?"*

- Answer "yes" + budget objection = real, work the procurement path
- Answer "I don't know" = trigger isn't salient, stop pushing
- Answer "no" = real disqualifier, exit cleanly

### 4. "Your data is too thin / not enough comparables."

**The underlying concern:** comparable-company integrity is Mento's moat — if they don't trust it, the deal dies.

**The response:** Show, don't tell.

> *"Fair pushback. Let me pull the 5 closest comparables to your shape right now and send you the band ranges. If they don't track to your gut, we shouldn't move forward."*

Send within an hour. The send itself is the test — if their gut tracks, the deal moves. If it doesn't, the deal was going to die anyway.

### 5. "Our CHRO wants to evaluate 3 vendors."

**The underlying concern:** standard enterprise procurement — usually means a long cycle.

**The response:** Don't fight it; structure it.

> *"That makes sense. We tend to be the fastest of the three to evaluate — 25-minute demo, 2-week trial on real data. Want me to map the comparison framework against what your CHRO is likely to score on?"*

Then send the framework. Becoming the one who *structures* the comparison usually wins more than being the one who refuses to be compared.

## What never to say

- *"That's a great question!"* (Nobody says this in a real conversation.)
- *"Let me get back to you on that."* (Always answer in the moment, even if the answer is "I don't know.")
- *"We're the only ones who..."* (Almost never true.)
- *"Trust me on this."* (If you have to ask for trust, you don't have it.)

## How to log objections

After every call that surfaces an objection:
1. Add a row to `accounts/<slug>/notes.md` with the objection + your response + their reaction
2. If a pattern emerges across 3+ accounts, propose an addition to this playbook
3. If the objection is one we haven't seen before, write it to `ideas/<slug>.md` and tag it for the next quarterly playbook update
